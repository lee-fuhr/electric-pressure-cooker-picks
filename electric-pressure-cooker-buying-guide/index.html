How to choose an electric pressure cooker

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

An electric pressure cooker is a countertop appliance that uses pressurized steam to cook food quickly, often cutting cooking times to a fraction of traditional methods. For most households, a 6-quart model with at least a sauté function and easy-to-clean nonstick pot offers the best balance of capacity, versatility, and value. Start with a well-reviewed mid-range model from a trusted brand, and you will likely use it more than your stovetop.

What size electric pressure cooker should I buy?

Size is the first decision because it affects both what you can cook and how much counter space you need. Cookers are typically measured in quarts or liters. A 3- to 4-quart model works well for singles or couples who mostly cook grains, soups, or small portions of meat. A 6-quart cooker is the most popular size: it fits a whole chicken, a decent batch of chili, or a hearty stew without being too bulky to store. For families of four or more, or if you like meal-prepping large batches, an 8-quart model gives you room for bigger roasts and multi‑serving casseroles. Keep in mind that you should never fill a pressure cooker more than two-thirds full for most foods (half for foamy foods like beans or oats), so a larger pot is safer when cooking ingredients that expand. The trade-off is that larger units take longer to come up to pressure and require more storage space.

What’s the difference between a pressure cooker and a multicooker?

A basic electric pressure cooker does one thing – cooks under pressure – but most modern models are actually multicookers that combine several functions in one appliance. A true multicooker typically includes pressure cooking, slow cooking, rice cooking, steaming, sautéing, and often yogurt-making, cake baking, or sous-vide modes. The terms are used almost interchangeably now, but if you see an appliance labeled only as a “pressure cooker,” check whether it also has a sauté or slow-cook setting. For a practical buyer, the versatility of a multicooker is worth the small premium. You can brown meat right in the pot before pressure cooking, simmer a sauce without the lid, or set it to slow-cook a stew while you are out. Models that also offer a dedicated steam function or a keep-warm timer add even more everyday usefulness. Just be wary of too many gimmick presets – the core functions that matter are: pressure cook (with adjustable time and pressure level), sauté, steam, slow cook, and keep warm.

Which features actually matter for daily use?

Beyond the basic functions, a few features separate a joy-to-use cooker from a frustrating one. An easy-to-read digital display with straightforward buttons (or a dial) matters more than a fancy touchscreen – touchscreens can be finicky with wet hands or steam. Look for adjustable pressure levels (high and low) and a custom or “manual” preset so you are not locked into automated programs that may not suit your recipe. A removable, nonstick inner pot is essential for quick cleanup, as food can stick if you don’t use enough liquid. Stainless steel pots are more durable and dishwasher-safe, but they may require a little more scrubbing for stuck-on bits. A pot with handles makes lifting heavy cooked dishes easier. Also check that the lid opens fully and locks securely, and that the steam release valve is easy to toggle between natural and quick release without burning your fingers. Finally, a quiet or muffled steam release is a nice touch in kitchens open to living spaces.

How much should I expect to spend on a good model?

Electric pressure cookers span a wide range, from budget-friendly models under a hundred dollars to premium machines that cost several hundred. The sweet spot for most people is the mid-range: these models offer solid build quality, a nonstick inner pot, multiple presets, and good safety certifications without extra frills you rarely use. Budget-friendly units can still cook well, but they often have less insulation (slower to come to pressure), flimsier lids, and fewer customization options. Premium models, such as those from well-regarded appliance brands, typically use thicker stainless steel pots, have programmable pressure levels, include a steam or sous-vide function, and offer better customer support or longer warranties. If you plan to use the cooker multiple times a week, spending a bit more for a durable model with a reputation for lasting many years is worthwhile. Avoid the cheapest no-name brands; their safety testing and temperature consistency can be unreliable.

What are the most reliable brands in 2026?

Instant Pot remains the benchmark for good reason – they pioneered the modern multicooker category and their 6-quart Duo and Duo Plus models are consistently praised for reliability, ease of use, and a huge community of recipes. Ninja and Breville make strong competitors that often offer more premium materials and additional functions like sous-vide or steam-crisping (in the case of Ninja Foodi). Cosori has emerged as a reliable mid-range option with clean displays and a solid build, especially recommended for those who prefer a wider, lower pot design. For those seeking a simple, no-frills cooker, brands like Crock-Pot and Cuisinart produce dependable models that lack smart features but focus on basic pressure and slow cooking. Stay away from generic unbranded units sold solely on online marketplaces; inconsistent pressure regulation and poor documentation make them a gamble. Always check recent owner reviews on multiple platforms to confirm long-term performance.

Are electric pressure cookers safe to leave unattended?

Yes, modern electric pressure cookers are designed with multiple safety mechanisms that make them safe to run while you do other things. They have locked lids that prevent opening until pressure is fully released, pressure sensors that shut off heat if pressure gets too high, and overheat protection that trips the unit if the bottom gets too hot. Most are UL or ETL certified, meaning they have passed independent safety tests. That said, you should never leave a pressure cooker unattended for hours on end, especially if you are using sauté mode which can burn food if dry. Quick-release steam is also very hot – keep children and pets away when releasing pressure. For overnight slow-cooking or long simmer cycles, read the manual carefully: some models automatically switch to keep-warm after a timer, others just turn off, which could leave food in the danger zone temperature-wise.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use my electric pressure cooker for canning?

Only if the manufacturer explicitly says the model is approved for pressure canning. Most standard electric pressure cookers do not reach or maintain the precise high pressure needed for safe low-acid canning (vegetables, meats). They can be used for water bath canning of high-acid foods like jams and pickles, but for shelf-stable canning of anything else, you need a dedicated pressure canner.

How long does it take to come to pressure?

It varies by model size and how full the pot is. A typical 6-quart cooker with a full load takes anywhere from 5 to 15 minutes to build pressure. Smaller loads and thinner liquids come to pressure faster. This pre-heating time is not included in the cooking time printed in recipes, so plan for an extra 10–20 minutes on top of the pressure-hold time.

Is the inner pot dishwasher safe?

Most inner pots are dishwasher safe, but check the manual. The nonstick coating on many budget models can degrade faster in the high heat and harsh detergents of a dishwasher; hand washing with a soft sponge is gentler and prolongs the pot’s life. Stainless steel pots generally hold up better in the dishwasher.

Can I cook frozen meat in an electric pressure cooker?

Yes, it is one of the best features of a pressure cooker. You can cook frozen chicken breasts, ground beef, or even whole roasts without thawing first. Just increase the cooking time by roughly 50% compared to fresh meat, and make sure you have enough liquid in the pot. Sautéing is not possible from frozen, but you can brown after pressure releasing if desired.

What size fits a family of four?

A 6-quart (about 5.7 liters) cooker is ideal for a family of four. It can hold a whole chicken, a large batch of soup, or a few pounds of stew meat. If you like making large portions for leftovers or meal prep, an 8-quart gives you more headroom. For two people or empty nesters, a 5-quart works fine.

Do I need to buy extra accessories?

Most cookers come with a steam rack, rice paddle, and measuring cup. A separate metal or silicone steam basket (depending on your pot’s coating) is useful for cooking eggs or vegetables without direct water contact. For yogurt makers, you may want a set of glass or plastic containers that fit inside the pot. Otherwise, you can cook a huge variety of meals with just the base unit and its included accessories.

In shortChoosing an electric pressure cooker comes down to matching size to your household, picking a model with the functions you’ll actually use, and trusting a brand with a proven track record. A 6-quart mid-range multicooker from a reliable brand will handle most everyday cooking tasks with less fuss than stovetop methods – and it may just become the hardest-working appliance on your counter.
